Habitat associations for species with few detections, North

It was not possible to create complex habitat association models for species detected fewer than 20 times in northern Alberta. For these species, a coarse index of habitat use was determined as the proportion of detections in each native vegetation and human footprint type in comparison to the proportional availability of the habitat types (see methods). An index value <1 indicates lower than expected proportion of detections in that habitat type; and index value >1 indicates a higher than expected proportion. Due to the coarseness of the analyses, maps of predicted habitat abundance were not created for these species.

Species–habitat associations in the Prairies and Parkland of Alberta

Models were used to show how species, relative abundance differed among soil and human footprint types in the grassland and parkland regions of Alberta (see methods). Models were only created for species that had at least 20 detections using data from ABMI. Due to natural disturbances and vegetation succession, varying amounts of aspen and other trees may be presentce on each soil type; the presence/absence of trees greatly affects the presence and abundance of many other biota. Predicted species abundance in each soil/human footprint type when trees were absent is shown with bars. Predicted abundance when trees were present is shown using diamonds either above or below the top of the bar. Vertical lines indicate 90% confidence intervals.

Predicted relative abundance throughout Alberta under reference conditions

Habitat association models, plus models describing how species varied spatially (taking into account the effect of surrounding human footprint) were used to predict species abundance in each quarter section (800 m x 800 m) under reference conditions (see methods). Predictions of relative abundance of species in each quarter section were made after all human footprint in the quarter section had been 'backfilled' based on native vegetation in the surrounding area. Quarter sections depicted in red were predicted to have the highest abundance for the species, grading through light tan to dark blue where the species was predicted to be less abundant or absent. This figure has uncertainty due to uncertainty in the models and in the underlying vegetation map (see uncertainty map below).

{% endif %} {% if page.toclabels.map-cr-all %}

Predicted relative abundance throughout Alberta under current conditions

Habitat association models, plus models describing how species varied spatially (taking into account the effect of surrounding human footprint) were used to predict species abundance in each quarter section under current conditions. Predictions of relative species abundance in each quarter section were made based on the vegetation and human footprint present in the quarter section in 2010. Quarter sections depicted in red were predicted to have the highest abundance for the species, grading through light tan to dark blue where the species was predicted to be less abundant or absent. This figure has uncertainty due to uncertainty in the models and in the underlying vegetation map.

{% endif %} {% if page.toclabels.map-df-all %}

Difference in relative abundance between current and reference conditions

For each quarter section the difference between predicted current and reference conditions was determined. In quarter sections depicted in green the species was predicted to have higher abundance under present conditions than under reference conditions, with the opposite true for quarter sections depicted in red. The intensity of green and red depict the relative magnitude of increase or decrease for the species between reference and current conditions. This figure has uncertainty due to uncertainty in the models and in the underlying vegetation map.

{% endif %} {% if page.toclabels.map-rf-cv %}

Uncertainty in predicted reference conditions

To highlight the degree of uncertainty in the models, we estimated the coefficient of variation for each township based on bootstrap predictions of reference abundance. Townships depicted in red have the highest coefficient of variation, while townships in dark green have the lowest.
